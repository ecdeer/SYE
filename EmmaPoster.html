Modeling Growth Rates of Fossilized Bivalves Using Bayesian Statistics

Emma Deering

Department of Math, Computer Science, and Statistics; St. Lawrence University; Advised by Dr. Matt Higham

Abstract

Exploring how growth rates of fossilized clams change across environmental conditions across Northern New York, specifically in regards to acidic vs non acidic bodies of water.

Key Objectives:

  • Use a Von Bertanfly growth model to model clam growth rates.

  • Estimate model parameters using a MCMC algorithm.

  • Incorporate a Bayesian framework to account for uncertainty and missing data.

Data

  • Using shell growth rings, the data records how much each clam grew during each annual growth period.

  • The data set contains the following useful variables:

    • Location: The lake or river that the clam lived in.

    • Growth Period: Indicates annual stage of growth.

    • Length: Overall length of shell

    • Growth: Total growth during that growth period

    • Body: Indicates if water body was a lake or river.

  • Data collected Dr. Judith Nagel-Myers of the St. Lawrence University (Geology).

  • 151 valves from 17 different localities.

Growth Model and Parameters

Von Bertanfly growth model models clam growth as length versus age, capturing decreasing growth rates over time typical of organism development.

\[ L(t) = L \cdot (1 - e^{-(k + \beta \cdot x) \cdot (t-t_0)}) \] where

  • \(t\) is time

  • \(L(t)\) is mean length at time t

  • \(L\) is the asymptotic length

  • \(k\) is the rate

  • \(\beta\) adjusts the growth rate based on acidity

  • \(x\) is binary that indicates acidity

  • \(t_0\) is time where length is 0

Take Aways

  • River clams have higher growth rates, max lengths, and sigma-squares than lake clams.

  • This differences in parameters mean that river clams grow at a faster rate and achieve a longer length overall than lake clams.

  • River water is less acidic than lake water. Clam shells are made out of calcium carbonate, which dissolve in acidic water. Differences in acidity between lake and rivers may partially explain the differences in shell length.
